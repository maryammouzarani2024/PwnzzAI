Introduction to the Sentiment Model

Before we dive in, let’s quickly review some fundamentals of Generative AI. Below are a few common questions you might have. If you’re already familiar with these concepts, feel free to skip ahead.

1. What is Gen AI and how it differs from AI?

Generative AI (Gen AI) refers to AI systems capable of creating new content—such as text, images, or music—rather than just analyzing data. Traditional AI focuses more on classification, prediction, or decision-making, while Gen AI emphasizes creativity and generation.

2. What is a LLM and how it is generated?

A Large Language Model (LLM) is a type of generative AI trained on massive text datasets to predict and generate human-like language. It is generated by training deep neural networks with billions of parameters using techniques like transformer architectures.

3. What are various ways to use Gen AI models in applications?

Gen AI models can be used for chatbots, content generation, summarization, code generation, creative writing, customer support, personalization, and more. They can be integrated via APIs or embedded directly into applications.

4. What are frontier models?

Frontier models are the most advanced AI models at the cutting edge of research and development. They often require significant computational resources to train and are capable of performing a wide range of complex tasks.

5. How LLMs generate a text?

LLMs generate text by predicting the next word (or token) in a sequence based on the context of the previous words. They use probability distributions learned during training to produce coherent and contextually relevant output.

6. What is a token?

A token is a unit of text used by language models. It may represent a word, part of a word, or even punctuation. Models process and generate tokens sequentially, which are then combined to form natural language text.

Logistic Regression Sentiment Model

If you’re new to machine learning and artificial intelligence, let’s start with a simple example: a sentiment analysis model. This will give you an intuition for what a model is— and introduce its most important building block: weights.

A sentiment analysis model classifies text based on the emotions or opinions it conveys. In our Pwnzza shop, for instance, we use it to analyze customer comments about our pizzas. By learning from both the words in each review and the associated customer ratings, the model figures out whether certain words are more likely to signal positive or negative sentiment.

During training, the model assigns weights to words: higher weights suggest a stronger positive sentiment, while lower (or negative) weights indicate a more negative tone.

In large language models (LLMs), this same idea scales dramatically. Instead of a handful of weights, LLMs contain billions or even trillions of them, spread across many layers and dimensions. This vast web of weights allows them to capture complex patterns, nuances, and relationships in language.
